Visual Perception and Perspective Representation

Fundamental Interaction of Color, Form, and Sahpe

Fundamental Interaction of Color, Form, and Shape is the introductory section of a three-part record on experimental investigations into the mechanics of color, form, and shape. We aim to systematize the notions perspective and perception computationally, for the purpose of designing coloroid substitution-cyphers with multi-language encryption.

Interaction of Color

In visual perception a color rarily appears as it is, that is to say, as it physically is. This makes color a relative medium; relative to electromagnetism, computation, and moreover calculation. To formalize a curriculum of color, means to dispose of spurious assumptions and deceptions exhaustively. To that end, this record begins with a protocol for developing color models/systems from experimentation. Rather than mechanically applying or only implying laws and rules of color harmony, distinct color effects are produced through discovery of the interaction of color; e.g., by letting pairs of distant-colors converge.

The primary aim of this program is to develop through experience, i.e., trial and error, a rigorous articulation of color. Plainly, this means prompting color-action and discerning color-relatedness. As a general training it means formal development of corpora on perspective representation and visual perception. Let us assume, therefore, rudimentary familiarity with optics, ocular physiology, and electromagnetism.

(Foundational premises)

1. Premise: Any given color C evokes an innumerable set of readings Cr = (r1, r2, ...).
2. Premise: Color ≠ shape ≠ form.
3. Premise: There are S states of color, where 2 ≤ S.
3.1. State: attributive
3.2. State: computational
4. CompUverse: Color:(shade, tone).

(Modes/models/systems)

RGB Additive coloring predicts the appearance of elicited colors by summation of numeric representations of directed lightwaves.
EM Subtractive coloring predicts the spectral power-distribution of light passing through layers of partially-absorbent media.

These parameters will underscore, annotate, and structure our intuition for the interdependence of color with form and placement; with quantity (meaning amount, respectively extension and/or number, including recurrence); with quality (intensity of light and/or hue); and with pronouncement (by joining or parting boundaries). The following exercises are presented and illustrated with expository annotation.

I.) Color recollection – visual memory

Suppose that a voice-activated computer monitor were instructed to project the color "red" to fifty viewers. Then the instructor has generated fifty-one unique instances of said color, and we can be sure that those productions differ. Even if we specify a color that each viewer has seen before, such as that of the Red Cross bannar. Even when all participants have hundreds of reds from which to select the Red Cross red, they will again choose distinctly. And even still; if that very flag were placed before them so that each may attend their focus to the same red, while each will recieve the same projection to their retina, no one can certify that they are perceiving and memorizing identically to another.

We can draw a pair of insights from this exercise regarding the perception and representation of color. First, distinct color memory is a natural limit of vision-mechanics and computing proficiencies available to the receiver. Second, nomenclature, i.e., lexical mapping is less efficient than bijective indexing.

2.) Color reading and contexture

There are two competing ideas in modernistic typography. One is that the simpler the form of a letter the simpler it's reading. The other states we pronounce letters but read words, as units, as Wordpictures. Both of these observations appear to gel with intuition. With a few good examples, it is quickly shown that words consisting of only capital letters present the most difficult reading; because of their dimensional uniformity.

When comparing serif characters with sans-serif, the former permits a more seamless reading. Contemporary predilections for sans-serif in text diverges from historical pragmatism. Sans-serifs were designed for pictorial captions, not for texts. Some authors even insist that such fonts make poor Wordpictures, and so influence the readers ability to clearly ascertain context from the source.
